SMARTSHEET

A Collaborative Work Management Tool

Smartsheet – A Collaborative Work Management Platform

Link: Smartsheet.com 

I currently work as a UX Designer for Smartsheet. Smartsheet is the world’s leading SaaS platform for managing and automating collaborative work, primarily serving the Enterprise market. In an industry where enterprise-grade products are generally complex and difficult to use, my role as a UX Designer is to present powerful capabilities of the product through an easy-to-use interface. I have worked on a number of product’s most requested and successful features.

 The following case study is a summary of the UX process I followed to design the #1 customer requested feature in the product – Multi Contact Column Type (the ability to assign multiple people to a task in a project). 

 

MY ROLE: 

Full-time UX Designer

 

TASKS:

User Research, Prototyping, Ideation,

Interaction Design, Usability Testing

 

Understanding Customer Needs

I kicked off the project by going through Smartsheet’s community post which has a list of all feature/enhancement requests submitted by customers. After analyzing customer comments through both user and business perspective, two key themes emerged:

(1) Multi Contact was considered a table stakes feature – several of Smartsheet’s competitors already had it

(2) Absence of this feature created a competitive gap and several customers refused to use the product because of it

 

Research Sprint Process

Along with UX Research and the Product Management team, we conducted a 5-day design sprint process. Through a card sorting exercise, we were able to group the key themes that emerged from customer interviews. By looking at larger product goals, customer needs and usability issues with the current product, we put prioritized features for MVP.

 

Rapid Prototyping & Testing

I adopted rapid prototyping and iterative testing method for coming up with a design solution. The first round of testing with low-fi prototypes indicated that users always chose Multi Contact option even though their use case was to assign only one user per task. Though it was great for discoverability and adoption of the new feature, it had an unintended consequence of a user always assigning multiple people, lacking clear accountability for completing a task.

 

Changing the Design Direction based on User Feedback

As a UX Designer, my goal was to not only focus on increasing feature adoption but design a solution that empowered a user to succeed in their overall goal. I decided to provide Multi Contact as a toggle within the existing column type. Even though the feature seemed less discoverable, the goal was to make users apply deeper thinking before choosing an option. I made the choice to default selection to Single Contact as it established accountability for completing tasks, promoting best project management practice. This design decision had the desired effect of users choosing the right option for their need.

 

Usability Test Findings

The next step was to evaluate the design by testing it with users. The testing revolved around perfecting several aspects of the design from interaction design, information hierarchy to the copy used. Following are the top three test observations and my proposed solution to it:

 

Faces or No Faces

I tested variants of the prototype with and without human faces. While some users felt adding face holders made the design noisy, there were other implicit benefits that surfaced during the discussion:

  • Use of human faces elicited emotional responses

  • Users attention gravitated to the images first and then to the content. This helped with quick scanning to understand resources allocated to each task

  • Human faces promoted trust and authority which was key to successful project management

 

High Fidelity Prototypes

As a final step, I came up with high fidelity mockups that aligned with the design patterns we had built for the product. I did an audit of the entire app to identify places where contact tokens and human faces were used and made sure the new design was applicable to other other scenarios as well.

 

Post Launch & Results

The feature was a success and was well received by current and new customers. The goal after launch was to have 10-50% of newly created contact columns to have Multi Contact feature enabled. We hit this goal quickly after launch, and it gradually continued to rise afterwards.

User feedback (Community, support and internal) indicated that turning Multi Contact ON/OFF was sometimes difficult or led to unexpected side effects for users. Though It looks like a simple control, it actually changed the column types, which had implications for reports, filters, etc. I worked with the team to ship enhancements for smooth toggling of single/multi contacts in the application.
