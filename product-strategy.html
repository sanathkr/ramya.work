PRODUCT STRATEGY

Life is too short to build something nobody wants..

Kards is an online wedding invitation company, focusing on the Indian Market.

Link: Kards.in 

Indian wedding invitation market has been stagnant for the past 20 years with majority of the purchases taking place offline, primarily through brick and mortar stores. It was time consuming, rigid, boring and old. As the founder of Kards, I saw an opportunity to change this. I designed and built an online customization platform to buy wedding invitations. You might be thinking – What is so great about this? It is kind of AWESOME because there were no such businesses catering to the modern Indian bride. It took 4 months and a team to convince a new age Instagram bride to make a purchase online through Kards.

Analyzing customer touch points

Kards had been in the market for about a year before I realized that the process was inefficient. As a first step, I started with mapping all the customer touch points from start to end. Then, I analyzed it step by step to identify points of frustration, steps that created a bottleneck and sub-steps that were taking up a lot of time behind the scenes.

 

The Vision

The bigger vision was to build Kards as a brand for buying quirky and modern Indian wedding invitations. In order to reach this goal, I broke it down into small-manageable projects.

1.     Let users design their own invite – Self serve options for last minute shoppers

2.     Allow users to share their designs digitally

3.     Reach our users through mobile

4.     Offer unique, fresh and quirky templates

 

Know thy Customer

Before I jumped into building the product, I spent time in extensive user research. Survey and interviews were our tools  of choice. I spoke to over 70+ potential users to understand the challenges faced by them, their needs and what makes a good “experience” for them. This research phase ended with giving shape to our ideal user (persona).

 

Know thy Competitor

Though the goal was to stay away from being a “me too” product, we learnt a great deal by studying our competitor’s products. Instead of just playing with other products out there, I also paid attention to less-obvious places like social media channels, blog comments, support pages etc. which turned out to reveal of wealth of information about what users liked/hated. This analysis, further enforced how we wanted to build and position our product.

 

Making sense of the Firehose of Ideas

At this point, I had distilled the product’s vision and gathered sufficient business intelligence to build the best product we thought will make a difference in the market. At this point, we had a fire hose of ideas. I decided to prioritize features that yielded maximum value to the user. I decided to focus on bigger items and strategic themes rather than details – “Develop a DIY platform”, “Improve mobile presence” and “Make users feel confident and empowered”

 

Execution - The Design & Development Phase

I realized that building a successful product was not a one women thing. I brought together a team necessary to make this a success.  Together, we came up with an achievable launch plan. We set an aggressive goal to design and build the product within 4 months. Our team had a Product designer and CEO (Me), a  Developer and Co-founder and Graphic designers. I was in charge of leading the design process from start to end.  I started with a mobile first approach and then moved on to larger screens. The design phase went from paper sketches, low fidelity design to high fidelity prototypes. I also conducted two rounds of usability testing to make sure the product delighted the target customer. The design was then developed and tested across platforms. More details on the design process is detailed out in a separate case study. 

 

Adapting from Actual Product Usage

Observing the actual product usage after launch pointed us to blockers that came in the way of making it a “successful launch”. Some users resorted to screenshot the designs instead of paying for it. Regarding pricing, few users found the cards expensive, while others found it cheap. After analysis, we segregated the designs into basic and premium ones and offered tiered pricing to capture audience across all levels. We also introduced watermarks to avoid plagiarism.  This fine tuning immediately showed an increase in sales.

 

Go to Market (GTM) and Pricing

As we were getting ready for the launch, we also started executing our plan to reach our target customers. Our GTM was three pronged – Partnering with influencers, developing a strong social media presence and generating organic traffic through blogs. We also resorted to a tiered pricing model to capture and cater to customers across different segments. This strategy helped us penetrate the market and turn heads.

 

Results

Yes, we got the product off the ground! The website was a great hit and received a lot of positive reviews from the users. Kards saw over 500 paying customers in the first month of launch. Kards was also featured in leading wedding planning websites as the go-to website to get quirky wedding e-cards.
